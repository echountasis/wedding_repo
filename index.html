Let's talk some Greek

Here are some easy words and phrases that you are definitely going to hear other people say; so why not to learn some and make an impression!?

Hello - Γειά σου (YAH-soo)

Use this to greet someone when you meet him/her. If it's a group and want to address them all you can say Γειά σας (YAH-sas)

Nice to meet you: Χάρηκα πολύ (HA-ree-ka po-LEE)

Usually we use this before we leave someone we just met, or when we intoduce each other.

How are you?: Tι κανείς (tee-KAH-nis)?

When you want to address a group of people you can ask Τι κανετε (tee-KAH-nete). We use the same phrase to ask someone what he/she is doing.

Good morning: Καλημέρα (kah-lee-MER-ah)

You would greet someone like this until noon (12:00pm). After that we ususally say “Γεια” (yah)

Good afternoon/evening: Καλησπέρα (kah-lee-SPER-ah)

You would use this greeting around 16:00 onwards, untill 19:00-20:00.

Goodnight: Καληνύχτα (kah-lee-NEEKH-tah)

We ususally use this when we say goodbye after we met someone and we are heading to bed.

Thank you: Ευχαριστώ (eff-kha-ri-STOE)

You can also say Ευχαριστώ πολύ (eff-kha-ri-STOE poli) which means Thank you very much.

Please/You’re welcome: Παρακαλώ (para-kah-LOE)

In Greek, the word for “please” and “you’re welcome” is the same, making it all the more easy to learn. It’s polite to say “para-kah-LOE” after asking for directions or the price of something. It can even be used to mean “I beg your pardon?” or “Huh?” when you’ve misunderstood or want someone to repeat something.

My name is... : Με λένε (may LEH-neh)...

This is to be used when you want oto indroduce yourself.

What is your name?: πως σε λένε? (pos-se LEH-neh)

This is the formal version of asking somoenes name, usually when we adress older people we are using the plural version out of respect which is What is your name?: πως σας λένε? (pos-sas LEH-neh)

Goodbye: Γειά σου (YAH-soo)

It is the same as the Hello (like italians use ciao for example) but the meaning change according to the situation

See/Talk to you later: Τα λέμε (tah-LEH-meh)

This is a very common way of closing a conversation.

Yes: Ναί (neh); No: όχι (OH-hee)

Be careful not to confuse yes and no — it's easy to mistakenly associate "neh" with "no" in English, and "oh-hee" with "okay" when in fact it's just the opposite! An easy mnemonic is to remember that they're actually the inverse of what you would initially think.

Excuse me/Sorry: Συγνώμη (See-GHNO-mee)

This word can be used in many different ways. You can use it to apologise to someone, get someone's attention in a polite way, say it to someone who blocks your way in order to pass etc..

Where is the bathroom?: Πού είναι η τουαλέτα (Poh-EE-nay ee tua-LEH-tah)?

Πού είναι (Poh-EE-nay) means Where is, you can use it whenever you want to ask someone the location of something.

Do you speak English?: Μιλάτε αγγλικά (Mee-LAH-teh ag-li-KAH)?

Usually in Greece people older than 50 do not speak English unless they are involved in the turism industry.

Cheers!: Στην υγειά μας! (STIN-eh YAH-mas)

This literally means "To our health!"; a must learn when you raise your glass and you address the people around you.

Bottoms up!: Ασπρο πάτο (AHS-pro PAH-toh)

Meaning literally "white bottom," if you use this with a new Greek acquaintance, you'll be sure to impress.

How much is it?: Πόσο κάνει αυτό (POH-soh KAH-nee af-TOH)?

This is used to ask the price of something. A useful word here is αυτό (af-TOH) which means that.

I don’t understand: Δεν καταλαβαίνω (Then Kah-tah-lah-VEH-noh)

For sure someone will speak to you Greek, so you can always reply that to him. At the end you can add φίλε μου (fy-le moo) which means my friend.

Help! Βοήθεια (voh-EE-thee-yah)

The most polite version of it, is to add Παρακαλώ (para-kah-LOE)

I love Greece: Αγαπώ την Ελλάδα (Ah-gah-POH teen Eh-LAH-tha)

A certain way to get a big hug, a kiss (pro COVID) or a cheers

Oops!: Ωπα (OH-pa)

Another word that has many meanings. Originally means woops; but you will hear it when people dance, or a car gets in front of them; generally is used for exclamation.

What's up/How's it going?: Τι λέει (tee-LEI)

This is used between friends as it is informal

What are you up to?: Που είσαι (pou-eeSAY)

Also it used to ask Where are you.

So good/so cool: και γαμώ (kay-gaMOU)

Although this is technically a curse word, you'll hear everyone using it colloquially to address friends. But only use it on someone you know!: μαλάκα (maLAka)

See you later: τα λέμε (ta LEH-meh)

This a way to say good buy again but in a more informal way

Dude/man: ρε φίλε (reh-FEEleh)

This is used to finish phrases in an informal way; for example Where are you re file?

Let's talk food

First things, first - Let's find the correct spot

While in Paros or any other greek island you would like to avoid restaurants. It's not that the food is bad; on the contrary the food is great, but fine dinning you can have in any place around the world. What we would recommend you is to enjoy as much as you can the taverns. You can find taverns next to the sea, literally where the wave breaks and also in the core of the villages. While you are at a place next to the sea you should avoid meat, the taverns specialty 99% is fresh fish. As you possible thought already, the taverns away from the sea are specialized in meat. You may see some animals (goats,chieckens etc next to the tavers - yes, the meat is fresh)

Now that we know where we eat; let's discuss about the culture

If we ever had the chance to have lunch or dinner at home together you would have seen that there are a lot of small plates in the middle of the table. This is how we eat at taverns. So our recommendation whould be to avoid taking e.g. a stake for you, but ideally you order a lot of small things all together and you share. Like that you are going to taste a lot of different plates and you will have the full experience

Starters

Don't eat the bread with the oil for starter; the good things are yet to come and you want your stomach to have available space. Definitily you will take a salad, that can be a Greek Salad or a Dakos. Then you will need some "sauces" (we don't really call them sauces). In this categorie you have tzatziki, taramosalata, melitsanosalata, turokauteri etc. All are delicious and you use the bread to eat them. Then we have the fried/ovened plates, these can be cheese pies, fired zucchini and eggplants, zucchine balls, meat balls, octopus with vinegar or stew, kalamaraki - make sure it's fresh, cheece saganaki, garides saganaki, sardines, gauros, maridaki etc.

Main Plates (optional)

Why is optional? It depends how many started you chose of course! If you followed our advice and share with your friends, the main plate is not going to be atomic. So what is the main plate? Usually the main plate is meat or fish in the middle to share. If you have choosen meat then lamp chops is one of the best choices. If you selected fish then you can either get a big fish to share (usually grilled) or many differnt small fish to share (red fish or similar). If you decided to take fish always ask what he got on the day; during summer taverns get fresh fish every morning and it's usual to ask them what is fresh.

Fast food - not really - we call it Souvlakia

We would not categorise it in the fast food but is definitely one of the best options for cheap, fast and delicious food. For ordering souvlakia follow the pattern

    • Select your meat (pork or chicke) - no lamp available.
      Pita or plate?
      Gyros (donner) or kalamaki (shish)
      If you selected pork then they usually add the below ingredients (fired fries, tomatoes, tzatziki, onions, red pepper)
      If you selected chicken then they usually add the below ingredients (fired fries, tomatoes, mayo based sauce, onions, lettuce red pepper)
      If you want all the ingredients you say "apo ola" it means "from everything", otherwise you specify what you don't want.

    • More traditional food

    Not that the previous is not traditional, but what we mentioned already is what Greeks eat when they go to the taverns. In their daily lives of course they don't go out every day (I think you have heard about recession). So we have really tasty traditional food that you can order. This could be fasolakia ladera, fakes, pastitsio, mousaka etc. I would propose you to ask them if they have any food of the "katasarola" - (caserole). Usually they prepare of the menu and they would be very happy to hear that you want something more traditional.

    Wait there is more?

    Yes, we didn't talk about deserts. Oh you will find some amazing precooked cakes, chocolates and frozen stuff - NOPE, sorry we lied. You will not find any of these. Usually desert is given for free when you are many people. It depends on the restaurant what it will bring, usually they have homemade yogurt (delicious) served with honey, or some fruity sauce (yes the one they have inherited from their gran-gran-gran-gran-gran-mother) or fresh fruits. In case they don't bring something you can always ask what do they have homemade and they will be glad to explain.

    Wrapping up

    Food is delicious and we are sure you will enjoy it. If you are vegetarian/vegan don't lose hope; the traditional/homemade foods category is for you, so feel free to ask them if they have any "ladera". If you opt in for the fish based mean you can either drink white wine (always caraf - don't buy a bottle) or you can try Ouzo. Be careful here, do not drink Ouzo when the sun is dirreclty on you - be under shade and be careful of the consuption. White wine is the way to go for most cases; yes it is light wine so feel free to enjoy it (always carefully). Also you will find a big varietty of beers (Fix, Alfa etc), if you ask the waiters for greek beers they will be glad to guide you through. Regarding water, in Greece generally if you go out for a coffee or food they bring water for free (unless it's bottled), feel free to ask them for a caraf of water which is free.
