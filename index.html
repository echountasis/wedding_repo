Let's talk some Greek

Here are some easy words and phrases that you are definitely going to hear other people say; so why not to learn some and make an impression!?

Hello - Γειά σου (YA-soo)

Use this to greet someone when you meet him/her. If it's a group and want to address them all you can say Γειά σας (YAH-sas)

Nice to meet you: Χάρηκα πολύ (HA-ree-ka po-LEE)

Usually we use this before we leave someone we just met, or when we intoduce each other.

How are you?: Tι κανείς (tee-KA-nis)?

When you want to address a group of people you can ask Τι κανετε (tee-KA-nete). We use the same phrase to ask someone what he/she is doing.

Good morning: Καλημέρα (ka-lee-MER-a)

You would greet someone like this until noon (12:00pm). After that we ususally say “Γεια” (yah)

Good afternoon/evening: Καλησπέρα (ka-lee-SPER-a)

You would use this greeting around 16:00 onwards, untill 19:00-20:00.

Goodnight: Καληνύχτα (ka-lee-NEEK-ta)

We usually use this when we say goodbye after we met someone and we are heading to bed.

Thank you: Ευχαριστώ (eff-ha-ri-STO)

You can also say Ευχαριστώ πολύ (eff-kha-ri-STO poli) which means Thank you very much.

Please/You’re welcome: Παρακαλώ (para-ka-LO)

In Greek, the word for “please” and “you’re welcome” is the same, making it all the more easy to learn. It’s polite to say “para-kah-LOE” after asking for directions or the price of something. It can even be used to mean “I beg your pardon?” or “Huh?” when you’ve misunderstood or want someone to repeat something.

My name is... : Με λένε (may LE-ne)...

This is to be used when you want to introduce yourself.

What is your name?: πως σε λένε? (pos-se LE-ne)

This is the formal version of asking somoenes name, usually when we adress older people we are using the plural version out of respect which is What is your name?: πως σας λένε? (pos-sas LEH-neh)

Goodbye: Γειά σου (YAH-soo)

It is the same as the Hello (like italians use ciao for example) but the meaning change according to the situation

See/Talk to you later: Τα λέμε (ta-LE-me)

This is a very common way of closing a conversation.

Yes: Ναί (ne); No: όχι (OH-hee)

Be careful not to confuse yes and no — it's easy to mistakenly associate "neh" with "no" in English, and "oh-hee" with "okay" when in fact it's just the opposite! An easy mnemonic is to remember that they're actually the inverse of what you would initially think.

Excuse me/Sorry: Συγνώμη (See-GHNO-mee)

This word can be used in many different ways. You can use it to apologise to someone, get someone's attention in a polite way, say it to someone who blocks your way in order to pass etc..

Where is the bathroom?: Πού είναι η τουαλέτα (Poh-EE-nay ee tua-LEH-tah)?

Πού είναι (Poh-EE-nay) means Where is, you can use it whenever you want to ask someone the location of something.

Do you speak English?: Μιλάτε αγγλικά (Mee-LA-te ag-li-KA)?

Usually in Greece people older than 50 do not speak English unless they are involved in the turism industry.

Cheers!: Στην υγειά μας! (STIN-eh YA-mas)

This literally means "To our health!"; a must learn when you raise your glass and you address the people around you.

Bottoms up!: Ασπρο πάτο (AS-pro PA-to)

Meaning literally "white bottom," if you use this with a new Greek acquaintance, you'll be sure to impress.

How much is it?: Πόσο κάνει αυτό (PO-so KA-nee af-TO)?

This is used to ask the price of something. A useful word here is αυτό (af-TO) which means that.

I don’t understand: Δεν καταλαβαίνω (Then Ka-ta-la-VE-noh)

For sure someone will speak to you Greek, so you can always reply that to him. At the end you can add φίλε μου (fy-le moo) which means my friend.

I love Greece: Αγαπώ την Ελλάδα (Ah-ga-PO teen Eh-LA-tha)

A certain way to get a big hug, a kiss (pre-COVID) or a cheers

Oops!: Ωπα (OH-pa)

Another word that has many meanings. Originally means woops; but you will hear it when people dance, or a car gets in front of them; generally is used for exclamation.

What's up/How's it going?: Τι λέει (tee-LEI)

This is used between friends as it is informal

See you later: τα λέμε (ta LE-me)

This a way to say goodbye again but in a more informal way

Dude/man: ρε φίλε (reh-FEEleh)

This is used to finish phrases in an informal way; for example Where are you re file?

Beaches

Paros is definitely known for its beautiful crystal clear beaches. You can find isolated coves where you can find complete privacy to long stretches of sand where you will find beach beds, umbrellas, watersports and cafés.

We suggest that you rent a car, so you will be able to reach anywhere at any time of the day. The island roads are pretty good compared to other islands and one can easily drive, as they are not so busy like in Athens. When roaming around with the car, you will definitely find some beaches that will be with no people at all that you can visit, but below we will be suggesting some of the more popular beaches that in our opinion they should not be missed.


1. Monastiri Beach

This beach is at the beginning of Paros Park. IT has the calmest clear waters we ever saw! It is shallow and serene! There are sunbeds and a nice beach bar, but you can always choose to lay on your towel under a tree. Last year the sunbeds were for free but some locals told us that they will not be so this year. This beach can get crowded during high season; for a quieter beach experience, check out Perikopetra or Turkou Ammos Beaches, just a short hike away and also in Paros Park. (https://www.parospark.com/paros-park-walking-routes/ )

2. Faragas Beach

This is a beautiful beach, with another great beach bar! The sunbeds here are quite expensive, as they are the cushioned ones. Again, you can always opt not to rent any sunbeds. Do not be tricked that you need to rent, as when you enter the beach you will need to pass from the beach bar.

3. Kolymbithres Beach

This is a must-see beach that is famous for its unusual lunar like granite rock formation. It is split in 2 parts and in between the rocks there are some sandy parts, some areas with sunbeds and some not. Roam around along the coast while swimming, to admire the rocky/sandy parts.

4. Kalogeros Beach

Would you like a free spa treatment? There is this small beach situated between two huge rock formations. As you approach the sand, take a right to claim a cute little cove, set your stuff UP on the rocks as there will be waves created by the passing ferries. Go to the rocks and scrape off some of the powder, mix it with sea water and rub it all over yourself. Let it dry and wash it off in the ocean.

5. Golden beach

It is a 700 meter long beach that got its name for its fine sparkly sand.

6. Santa Maria beach

It is mostly famous for its beach bars and their music

Let's talk food

First things, first - Let's find the correct spot

While in Paros or any other Greek island ideally you avoid restaurants and opt for local traditional tavernas. It's not that the food is not great, but restaurants serve typical European food that you can find anywhere in Europe (like burgers, pasta etc). There are plenty of tavernas, especially on the islands, and you can find them either next to the sea, literally where the wave breaks or in the village core, in the narrow-whitewashed streets. When you are at tavernas next to the sea, we suggest that you will opt for fish, as usually seaside taverns specialize in fresh fish, but if you do not like fish, do not worry, as they will always have other options! While seaside tavernas specialize in fish, usually other tavernas away from the sea specialize in meat, and sometimes you can see that they will have their farms close by with goats, chickens, etc.

Now that we know where we eat; let's discuss about the culture

If we ever had the chance to have lunch or dinner, at home together, you would know that we usually serve a variety of dishes and put them in the middle for everyone to share. This is how we eat at taverns, so our recommendation would be to order a lot of small plates and you share each plate. Like that you are going to taste a lot of different plates and you will definitely immerse yourself into the full Greek experience.

Starters

Try to avoid taking bread to start with as you want your stomach to have available space, for all the delicious ‘meze’. Greeks specialize in salads and they usually order it at the beginning with all the small plates, for salads you can either opt for the traditional Greek Salad, or else you can order Dakos, that will be served on a Cretan bread at the bottom (the bread at the bottom is baked twice, that is why it is hard). For both salads the main ingredient is Feta or Mizithra cheese (mizithra is softer than feta and it is usually made from goats’ milk, and it is closer to ricotta as a texture) As a side you can also order some ‘sauces/dips’ (we do not really call them that way); like tzatziki, taramosalata, melitzanosalata, turokauteri, etc.. all are delicious, and you can dip bread or potatoe fries in all of them. Now you would also need some fried/oven baked plates. Some that we suggest are fried zucchini, fried eggplants, fried feta with honey and sesame, zucchini balls, meat balls, cheese pies, cheese saganaki, octopus with vinegar or stew, kalamarakia (make sure to ask whether its fresh), garides saganaki (shrimps baked with ouzo in salsa with crumbled feta - make sure to dip your bread in this sauce), sardines, gauros, kolios, maridaki, etc.

Main Plates (optional)

Why is this optional? It depends on how many starters you will choose! Some will prefer to order starters only, but if you would like some meat, or bigger portions of fish you can still order a main plate and share it in the middle. If you would like meat, lamb chops are definitely one of the best choices, and if you would like to order fish you can always ask what the fresh fish of the day is (eg: seabream, mullet, stuffed calamari, etc), or order smaller fish to share like mentioned in the starter section.

Fast food - not really - we call it Souvlakia

We would not categorize it in the fast food but is definitely one of the best options for cheap, fast and delicious food. To help you in ordering souvlakia you first need to select your meat (pork of chicken – lamb is not available), then choose whether it is in a pitta or in a plate – Gyros (is like doner) and Kalamaki (is like kebab/shish). If you select pork they usually add fried fries, tomatoes, tzatziki, onions and red pepper. If you select chicken they usually add fried fries, tomatoes, mayo based sauce, onions, lettuce and red pepper. If you want all the ingredients you say “apo ola” it means “from everything” otherwise you specify what you don’t want.

More traditional food - mostly served at home

Not that the previous is not traditional, but what we mentioned already is what Greeks eat when they go to the taverns. In their daily lives they eat other tasty traditional food that you can also order in taverns. This could be fasolakia ladera (beans with salsa), fakes, pastitsio (similar to lasgna), mousaka etc. You can ask them if they have any food of the "katasarola" - (casserole). Usually, they prepare them, off the menu and they would be very happy to hear that you want something more traditional.

Wait there is more? … Desserts

Yes, we didn't talk about desserts. Usually, desert is given for free when you order many plates to share for main course. It depends on the taverna, of what it will bring, usually they have homemade yogurt (delicious) served with honey, or some fruity sauce (yes, the one they have inherited from their gran-gran-gran-gran-gran-mother) or fresh fruits. In case they don't bring something, you can always ask what homemade desserts do they have, and they will be glad to explain.

Wrapping up

Food is delicious and we are sure you will enjoy it. If you are vegetarian/vegan don't lose hope; the traditional/homemade foods category is for you, so feel free to ask them if they have any "ladera". If you opt in for the fish based mean you can either drink white wine (ideally choose the carafe – it is easier to drink and way cheaper) or you can try Ouzo. Be careful here, as it is quite strong (approx. 40%). You can also find a big variety of beers (Fix, Alfa etc), if you ask the waiters for greek beers they will be glad to guide you through. Regarding water, in Greece generally if you go out for a coffee or food they bring water for free (unless it's bottled), feel free to ask them for a carafe of water which is free.

Meet the Bride & Groom

It was 2017 when Manolis and Elaine's love story first began, two single twenty-somethings crossed paths at work, the next day they both swiped right, and on the third day they met up for their first date at Birgu waterfront.

A week later Manolis had to go back to Australia where he was working at that time. Knowing he had his brother’s wedding in a months’ time in Greece, Elaine decided to go meet Manolis in Greece and be the plus one for the wedding ... little did Elaine know that she will meet ALL the family and be greeted with so much love and warmth.

The little adventure ended up being a trip that made Elaine and Manolis realize that there is something more than just a fling. After discovering the love, they both share for food and travel, it wasn’t long before Manolis and Elaine moved in together, and their whirlwind romance began! From surfing classes in Portugal, to skating in Croatia, to sipping mulled wine in the Christmas markets of Germany, and enjoying wonderful trekking memories in Cornwall, they both seek to create special moments together. In between travel, Greece will always be their second home to cherish family moments like birthdays and Christmas and to take their dose of delicious Greek food and experience beautiful new places.

Elaine and Manolis are now so looking forward to starting this new chapter together, with many more experiences to come. They cannot wait to celebrate the beginning of this adventure with all of their favorite people at their second home in Greece.

How to get to Paros island?

You can visit Paros by ferry or by plane. The ferry leaves from Piraeus port or Raffina Port. If time is not a problem, it is better to go by ferry, as you get to enjoy the view from the deck. The best platform for ferry tickets is Viva. It is straightforward and user friendly to check prices and book online tickets. There is also a small airport, which you can fly into from Athens airport. The flight lasts around 45 mintues'
